About the data

What is an MRP?

A standard poll of ~2,000 people can't say anything reliable about a single constituency. Multilevel Regression and Post-stratification (MRP) solves this by fitting a demographic model of vote choice on a much larger sample (10k–40k), then post-stratifying that model across each constituency's census makeup. MRPs called Labour's 2017 underperformance and the Conservatives' 2019 majority more accurately than conventional poll aggregation.

How is the win probability calculated?

Neither MRP publishes posterior draws — just point estimates. The probability is a Normal-CDF proxy on Reform's mean margin over its top competitor:

P(Reform wins) = Φ(mean_margin × 100 / σ)

σ is derived empirically from how much the two MRPs disagree on each seat's margin — not a fixed assumption. A per-seat σ is built from a whole-set floor (≈6.4 pp, from cross-pollster RMS disagreement of ≈9 pp) plus that specific seat's pollster disagreement. Seats where the MRPs closely agree get tighter probabilities; seats where they diverge slide toward 50%.

What's the 2024 baseline used for?

Actual Reform UK vote share from the July 2024 general election (HoC Library data), used to compute each seat's swing from 2024 → 2026 MRP mean. This turns absolute vote share into a more interpretable "Reform gained / lost X pp here since the last election."

Caveats
  • No Northern Ireland — neither MRP models NI's 18 seats (shown grey on the map).
  • No Chorley — the Speaker's seat is uncontested by major parties and excluded from both MRPs.
  • Not a forecast. Two MRP snapshots averaged — a read of early-2026 voting intention, not a call on the next election.
  • Not the MRPs' own posterior. A Normal-margin proxy still overstates certainty in very safe seats and doesn't account for correlated national errors.
  • The 3-month gap between MiC (Apr 2026) and EC (Jan 2026) means part of the cross-pollster disagreement is real polling movement, not methodology noise. A fresher second MRP would tighten uncertainty.
